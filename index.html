The qualities that a designer should have are
  1. Creativity: The ability to think outside the box and generate unique, innovative ideas is essential for a designer. Creativity drives originality in design solutions.
  2. Attention to Detail: Designers must focus on every small detail, ensuring that their designs are precise, clean, and professional.
  3. Problem-Solving Skills: Designers often address complex issues through their designs. They need strong problem-solving skills to find effective solutions that meet the client's needs.
  4. Technical Skills: A designer must have proficiency in design software (such as Adobe Creative Suite, Figma, or AutoCAD) and a solid understanding of design principles like typography, color theory, and layout.
  5. Communication: Effective communication with clients, team members, and stakeholders is vital to understand the project's requirements and present design concepts clearly.
  6. Adaptability: The design world is ever-changing, and a good designer should be able to adapt to new trends, tools, technologies, and project needs.
  7. Time Management: Designers often juggle multiple projects with tight deadlines. Good organizational and time management skills are crucial for meeting those deadlines while maintaining quality.
  8. Empathy: Understanding the needs and preferences of the target audience helps designers create more user-friendly, appealing, and functional designs.
  9. Collaboration: Many design projects require teamwork. A good designer should be able to collaborate with other professionals such as developers, marketers, and clients.
  10. Curiosity and Continuous Learning: The design industry is always evolving. A great designer stays curious and continuously seeks to improve their skills and knowledge by learning about new tools, trends, and technologies.

In this session, we learned about the steps in Design Thinking. The five steps of Design Thinking are:

1. Empathize: Understand the users and their needs through research, observation, and direct engagement. This step is focused on gaining deep insights into the challenges users face, and it serves as the foundation for all subsequent steps.

2. Define: Clearly articulate the problem based on the insights gathered in the Empathize phase. This step involves pinpointing the core issue or challenge that needs to be addressed, ensuring that the problem is well-defined and understood.

3. Ideate: Generate a wide range of creative solutions to the problem. Brainstorming sessions and exploring different approaches are crucial in this phase to develop innovative ideas that could potentially solve the problem.

4. Prototype: Create simple, tangible representations of the ideas, such as models or mockups. These prototypes are used to visualize potential solutions, test different concepts, and identify areas for improvement.

5. Test: Test the prototypes with real users to gather feedback and insights. This stage helps refine the solution, validate its effectiveness, and determine how well it addresses the defined problem.

Design Thinking is not a linear process. For example, after testing a prototype, you may return to the Ideate phase to rethink or modify solutions. During the Empathize phase, you might identify new problems that require redefining the challenge.

--- Stakeholder Types:

There are three types of stakeholders in a project:

1. Primary Stakeholders: These are individuals or groups who are directly affected by the organization‚Äôs actions, such as employees, customers, or patients. They experience the direct impact of the decisions made.

2. Secondary Stakeholders: These are individuals or groups who are indirectly affected by or have an influence on the organization. For example, suppliers, partners, or regulators who may not be directly impacted but still play a role in the organization‚Äôs operations.

3. Tertiary Stakeholders: These are external groups or individuals who are marginally involved or affected by the organization, such as industry regulators, competitors, or advocacy groups. Their influence is usually more peripheral.

--- Types of Research:

1. Primary Research: This involves collecting original data directly from sources through methods such as surveys, interviews, observations, or experiments. It provides firsthand information that is directly relevant to the project.

2. Secondary Research: This involves gathering data that has already been collected and published by others, such as academic papers, market reports, or industry studies. It helps in understanding broader trends and gathering background information without primary data collection.

In today's design thinking session, our group was ready to present our presentation. However, due to time constraints, the professor was unable to review it. Instead, the session focused on an in-depth discussion of the concepts of secondary research and primary research.
Secondary research involves collecting data from existing sources such as newspaper articles, journals, reports, or reliable websites. It is used to validate findings, understand background information, or identify trends. However, the professor emphasized that secondary research should rely on credible sources. Websites like Quora or review platforms are not considered reliable because they often contain subjective opinions or unverified information. Instead, secondary research should come from trusted sources like government reports, scientific studies, academic journals, or reputable news outlets.
The professor emphasized that secondary research adds depth and context to a project, complementing and supporting primary research findings. This session enhanced our understanding of how to approach research systematically and filter information effectively to ensure credible results. It also highlighted the importance of properly citing sources to maintain academic and professional integrity.

Additionally, the session introduced us to several essential tools and techniques for structured and empathetic problem-solving. Here's a detailed overview of what we learned:

Stakeholder Map

Vignesh Sir introduced the concept of the Stakeholder Map, a visual tool that illustrates the interconnections between various stakeholders within a system or organization. Stakeholders can include individuals, groups, or entities that influence or are affected by a specific problem or process. To clarify the concept, he used the example of a Dairy Milk company. He explained how stakeholders such as farmers (who supply milk), manufacturers (who process the product), distributors, retailers, and customers all play interconnected roles, forming a dynamic ecosystem that sustains the business.
We were guided on creating our own Stakeholder Map for the problem we are addressing. For example, in our project on government hospitals, stakeholders could include doctors, nurses, patients, cleaning staff, administrators, and government bodies. By mapping out their roles and connections, we gained insights into their influence on the system and how they are affected by or contribute to the problem.

Why Web Map

We also learned about the Why Web Map, a tool that helps uncover the root cause of problems by repeatedly asking "Why." The process starts with identifying the main issue and progressively asks why the problem exists, digging deeper into its underlying causes.

For example, if the issue is "Patients face delays in receiving treatment," the questioning process might unfold as follows:

1. Why do patients face delays? ‚Üí Because there is a shortage of doctors.
2. Why is there a shortage of doctors? ‚Üí Due to limited recruitment opportunities.
3. Why is recruitment limited? ‚Üí Because of insufficient government funding.
This iterative questioning process continues until we reach the root cause, allowing us to focus on solving the fundamental issue instead of just treating the symptoms.

Empathy Map

Lastly, our teacher introduced the Empathy Map, a tool designed to help us understand and empathize with the experiences and emotions of our stakeholders. The map is divided into key sections, each focusing on different aspects of the stakeholder's perspective.
1. What do they feel?

What are the emotions they experience?

Are they stressed, frustrated, happy, or relieved?

1. What do they hear?

What kind of feedback or conversations are influencing them?

Are they hearing complaints, encouragement, or instructions?

1. What do they see?

What does their environment look like?

Are there overcrowded waiting rooms, unhygienic conditions, or helpful resources?

1. What do they say?

What are they verbally expressing about their situation?

Are they voicing concerns, gratitude, or suggestions for improvement?

This method helps us deeply understand the stakeholder‚Äôs perspective, which is essential for developing solutions that are user-focused and practical.

Summary

The Stakeholder Map, Why Web Map, and Empathy Map are powerful tools that provide a structured approach to understanding both the problems at hand and the people affected by them. They guide us in thinking critically while fostering empathy, helping us develop human-centered solutions.
